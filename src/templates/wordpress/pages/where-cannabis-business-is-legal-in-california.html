Cannabis is legal in California. But cities and counties can prohibit cannabis businesses. This creates a patchwork of areas where it is and is not legal to establish a cannabis business. As a result, many people cannot get safe, tested cannabis products where they live. And the illegal market continues to thrive.

Why cannabis businesses are not legal everywhere

The state allows these types of cannabis businesses to operate:

  • Cultivation
  • Manufacturing
  • Testing laboratories
  • Distribution
  • Retail

But each city or county can decide whether to allow these business types in their area. They can:

  • Allow all cannabis businesses
  • Allow some types and prohibit others
  • Prohibit all cannabis businesses

In fact, 56% of cities and counties prohibit all cannabis businesses. And 62% prohibit cannabis retailers. Many cities and counties made these prohibitions after cannabis use became legal.

Why cities may have different rules than their county

Counties can decide which types of cannabis businesses they allow or prohibit. But county rules only apply to the unincorporated parts of the county. They do not apply to incorporated cities that are within the county. 

Incorporated cities can decide which types they allow or prohibit within their city. Their rules do not need to align with the rules of the county that they are in.

Some cities and counties have not made their own rules about cannabis businesses. These cities and counties follow state rules.

More limits on cannabis businesses

Some cities and counties allow a type of cannabis business, but also put restrictions on it. For example, they may limit the number of businesses that can get a license. 

Due to these limits, there are less than 4 retail licenses per 100K people in California. By comparison, there are:

  • 18 licensed retailers per 100K people in both Oregon and Colorado
  • 20 licensed retailers per 100K people in Alaska

The DCC compiled these figures in March 2022 using data available online.

How restrictions on cannabis businesses affect Californians

Two-thirds of cities and counties prohibit all cannabis retail. These communities miss out on economic development and job creation. 

Almost half of Californians cannot buy cannabis products that:

  • Are sold by a local licensed retailer
  • Meet strict requirements for safety
  • Have passed testing for harmful contaminants
  • Are marketed or sold in ways that do not put children at risk

And people who need it for a medical condition cannot get it from a local licensed business.

When people cannot buy it from a licensed business, they may turn to the illegal market to meet their needs. As a result, the illegal market continues to thrive. And licensed businesses struggle to stay afloat amid competition from the illegal market.
